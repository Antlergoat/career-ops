# Anti-AI Writing Style Skill

## Purpose

Make prose sound like a capable human wrote it with real context, judgment, and restraint. Avoid the common tells associated with generic AI prose: inflated significance, vague attribution, over-balanced structure, repetitive sentence patterns, formulaic conclusions, and over-polished filler.

This skill is not about hiding authorship. It is about improving writing quality: specificity, accuracy, natural cadence, useful detail, and appropriate voice.

## Use this skill when

Use this skill before producing or revising any prose intended for publication, sharing, client review, internal documentation, marketing, education, outreach, or long-term reuse.

Use it especially when the user asks for writing that is:

- Natural, human, plainspoken, editorial, grounded, or non-AI-sounding
- Less salesy, less fluffy, less corporate, less generic, or less polished
- Similar to a brand voice, source document, interview transcript, or human draft
- Fit for a wiki, knowledge base, website, article, email, presentation script, proposal, or social post

Do not use this skill for code-only tasks unless the task includes prose comments, README copy, docs, commit messages, release notes, issue descriptions, or user-facing text.

## Core rule

Prefer concrete claims over generalized importance.

A strong sentence usually does one of these:

- Names a specific fact
- Explains a real cause or consequence
- Makes a useful distinction
- Gives a clear instruction
- Connects directly to the reader's problem
- Preserves a human observation from the source material

A weak AI-sounding sentence usually does one of these:

- Declares that something is important without proving it
- Uses abstract nouns instead of concrete details
- Smooths over uncertainty or missing evidence
- Adds a tidy conclusion that the source material does not support
- Sounds broadly applicable to almost any subject

## Workflow

Before writing:

1. Identify the real task, audience, medium, and stakes.
2. Pull forward concrete source details: names, dates, materials, product facts, constraints, exceptions, user language, and operational realities.
3. Decide the appropriate register: plain, technical, promotional, advisory, conversational, or formal.
4. Remove the default essay shape unless the medium truly requires it.

While writing:

1. Start with the useful point, not a throat-clearing summary.
2. Use direct subject-verb-object sentences when possible.
3. Vary sentence length naturally, but do not force drama.
4. Keep transitions functional. Do not over-signpost.
5. Use industry terms where they add precision. Avoid jargon where it only decorates.
6. Preserve uncertainty where it matters, but do not wrap every claim in hedging.
7. Do not invent significance, history, market impact, consensus, or broad trends.

After writing, run the audit checklist below and revise once before returning the final copy.

## Avoid these AI-writing tells

### 1. Inflated significance

Avoid phrases that make ordinary facts sound historically important:

- plays a crucial role
- serves as a testament to
- underscores the importance of
- marks a pivotal moment
- reflects a broader trend
- represents a significant shift
- continues to shape the landscape
- leaves an indelible mark
- stands as a reminder
- deeply rooted in

Replace with the actual fact, mechanism, or consequence.

Bad:
The vent plays a crucial role in protecting homes and reflects a broader shift toward wildfire resilience.

Better:
The vent blocks embers at common attic and crawlspace openings, where wind-driven debris can enter before flames arrive.

### 2. Generic praise and puffery

Avoid unsupported adjectives:

- innovative
- cutting-edge
- groundbreaking
- world-class
- robust
- seamless
- powerful
- transformative
- best-in-class
- revolutionary

Use them only when the claim is sourced, measurable, or clearly a brand-approved phrase.

Bad:
Our innovative solution provides robust protection for modern homeowners.

Better:
The assembly combines stainless ember mesh with an intumescent matrix designed to restrict ember and flame entry during wildfire exposure.

### 3. Vague attribution

Avoid vague social proof:

- experts say
- many believe
- research shows
- industry leaders recognize
- widely regarded as
- media outlets have covered
- studies suggest

Name the source, standard, organization, person, publication, or dataset. If the source is not available, say less.

Bad:
Experts recognize defensible space as an essential part of wildfire preparation.

Better:
California's Zone 0 requirements focus on keeping the first five feet around a structure noncombustible.

### 4. Formulaic contrast patterns

Avoid repetitive constructions:

- not only X but also Y
- more than just X
- from X to Y
- whether X or Y
- both X and Y
- as much about X as it is about Y

These patterns are not banned, but they become obvious when repeated. Rewrite with direct claims.

Bad:
Home hardening is not only about materials but also about creating a complete wildfire defense strategy.

Better:
Home hardening works best when vents, gutters, siding gaps, decks, and the first five feet around the home are addressed together.

### 5. Rule-of-three padding

Avoid automatic three-item lists when two items are enough or when the third item is filler.

Bad:
This approach improves safety, efficiency, and peace of mind.

Better:
This approach reduces ember entry points and gives contractors a clearer retrofit path.

### 6. Generic concluding paragraphs

Do not end with a sweeping recap unless the format requires it. Avoid endings like:

- In conclusion
- Ultimately
- As technology continues to evolve
- As wildfire risks continue to grow
- By understanding these factors
- This highlights the importance of

End on the most useful next step, concrete implication, or final specific point.

### 7. Over-structured formatting

Avoid making everything a symmetrical outline. Do not overuse:

- Bold lead-ins on every bullet
- Emoji headings
- Excessive tables
- Repeated section intros and outros
- Horizontal rules
- Title Case headings when sentence case fits better
- Nested bullets that make simple prose look like a template

Use structure only when it helps the reader scan or act.

### 8. Synthetic balance

Do not force both-sides framing when the evidence or task does not call for it.

Bad:
While this product offers many benefits, it also raises important considerations.

Better:
Use stainless steel near coastal environments. Galvanized steel is more vulnerable to corrosion in salt-air exposure.

### 9. Hollow reader reassurance

Avoid generic service language:

- It is important to note
- It is worth mentioning
- This guide will explore
- Let's dive in
- You're in the right place
- We understand that every project is unique

Replace with substance.

Bad:
It is important to note that every project is unique.

Better:
Retrofit projects usually start with the existing vent size, wall condition, and whether the opening can be accessed from inside the attic or crawlspace.

### 10. Unnatural polish

Leave room for normal human rhythm. Not every sentence needs to be maximally smooth. Avoid sanding away useful specificity, mild asymmetry, or practical phrasing from source material.

## Preferred writing behaviors

### Be specific early

Open with the concrete point.

Instead of:
Wildfire safety is an increasingly important concern for homeowners across the western United States.

Use:
Wind-driven embers can enter attics, crawlspaces, and soffit cavities through vents long before the flame front reaches the house.

### Use source-shaped language

When working from notes, interviews, transcripts, or existing brand copy, preserve the user's most precise phrasing unless it is inaccurate, awkward, or off-brand.

### Prefer earned claims

A claim is earned when it is supported by one of the following:

- A standard, code, test, or listing
- A named source
- A measurable product attribute
- A documented customer or operational reality
- A clear causal mechanism
- A constraint the reader recognizes

### Cut generic abstractions

Watch for words like:

- landscape
- journey
- realm
- space
- ecosystem
- framework
- narrative
- tapestry
- intersection
- dynamic
- holistic
- comprehensive
- meaningful
- impactful

These words are allowed only when they are the most precise word available. Usually they are not.

### Use plain transitions

Prefer:

- But
- So
- Because
- For example
- In practice
- The catch is
- That matters because

Avoid ornate transitions when simple ones work.

### Keep the user's level of confidence

If the user is cautious, do not overstate. If the user has a defensible claim, do not weaken it unnecessarily.

## Revision pass

Before final output, check the draft against these questions:

1. Could this paragraph apply to dozens of unrelated companies, products, people, or topics?
2. Did I add importance, legacy, trend, or transformation language that the source did not prove?
3. Are there vague attributions where a named source should appear?
4. Are there three-part lists where one item is filler?
5. Did I use repeated sentence templates?
6. Did I overuse bold, tables, title case, or bullet symmetry?
7. Does the ending say something useful, or does it merely summarize itself?
8. Did I preserve the real technical, operational, or human detail?
9. Is the tone appropriate to the medium, or did it default to corporate-explainer voice?
10. Would a knowledgeable human in this field actually write it this way?

If the answer exposes a problem, revise before responding.

## Output requirements

When drafting new copy:

- Provide only the finished copy unless the user asks for commentary.
- Do not announce that you avoided AI writing.
- Do not mention this skill.
- Do not include a checklist unless requested.

When editing existing copy:

- Preserve meaning unless asked to rethink strategy.
- Improve specificity and rhythm.
- Remove unsupported claims.
- Keep the user's voice where it is already working.
- Return the revised version first, followed by brief notes only if useful.

When reviewing copy:

- Identify the highest-risk AI tells first.
- Quote only short snippets.
- Explain what to change and why.
- Offer a revised version of the worst section when practical.

## Strong default style

Use a clear, grounded, human editorial voice:

- Concrete over abstract
- Specific over sweeping
- Accurate over impressive
- Useful over polished
- Natural over symmetrical
- Medium-aware over template-driven
